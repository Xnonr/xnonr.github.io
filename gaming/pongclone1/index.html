AI Ball Path Prediction Modeling Within Pong Clone

Using Machine Learning Within R Language

Overall Goal

  • To develop a more intelligent, efficient and ‘human like’ Predictive Opponent AI.

Background Information

  • The current Simple Opponent AI in both the Classic and Advanced game modes ‘cheats’ in that it knows where the ball is located at all times, much like a human player does with their eyesight, yet in contrast can move perfectly as needed every time so as to never miss, unlike humans’ inevitable mistakes, limited reaction and manual keyboard input times.
  • The Simple Opponent AI has the paddle or paddles, depending upon not only the number of players but also the selected game mode, ‘chase’ after the ball in a simple fashion, where upon hitting it once it will return to a sort of standby mode, yet still moving up or down depending on said ball’s current ‘y’ coordinate location within the game window or playing field, ensuring it may be able to intercept the next incoming hit with all and best possible haste.
  • The Simple Opponent AI is only beatable due to artificial limits imposed upon the speed at which it can move the paddles every so often, in order to more closely resemble human and physical hardware (i.e. keyboard) input capabilities, otherwise it would be unbeatable whenever facing a human player. Against itself, often a stalemate will emerge, with neither side scoring any points or very few points being scored yet the round itself ultimately still ending in a tie.

Predicting the Ball’s Movement & Path: Logic & Considerations

  • The purpose of the new Predictive Opponent AI is to limit the computer’s aforementioned ‘cheating’ to a bare minimum. Therefore, all calculations are initially based off of the oncoming ball’s original location and velocities, both in terms of ‘x’ and ‘y’ coordinates, only during a very small window and frame of time, specifically just as is crossing over the dividing line on the playing field.
  • For our current purposes, we are only looking as far as the Classic game mode, where the paddles remain at a fixed ‘x’ coordinate and may move along the ‘y’ axis, taking into consideration only the height at which the paddles must be upon their side of the screen or playing field in order to prevent an opposing goal, intercepting and thereby redirecting the ball. This limits the complexity of the problem to only 1 dimension of movement with reference to the paddles, but not for that of the ball.
  • Predicting where the ball would ‘score’ if deliberately let through makes up the majority of this section’s challenge. As of now, the speed of the ball in terms of its ‘x’ velocity is fixed, as well as when it is considered as having scored upon reaching an ‘x’ value of either -390 or 390 (The entire screen or playing field is 800x by 600y, its center starting at point (0, 0)). Predicting the desired ‘y’ value is where the majority of the complexity is contained.
  • If the time it takes for the ball to reach either the top or the bottom of the screen is longer than it does to reach either the right or left edges, then the math is simple. Simply take the ‘y’ velocity of the ball, multiply it by the time its takes to reach the end of the field divined from the ‘x’ velocity and add the resulting product to its starting ‘y’ position and you are golden.
  • If it is the other way around, then prediction becomes much more difficult. Due to the different angles at which the ball can bounce off either paddles, different ‘y’ velocities can emerge, whose values are changed in order to simulate said angles. The ball could bounce, once, twice or several times across both the top and bottom borders of the field changing its ‘y’ velocity each time to be either positive or negative, though its absolute value would remain the same.
  • The distance that the ball covers in ‘y’ must therefore be determined, allowing us to discovered how many times it will bounce, where it bounces last, either at the top or bottom of the screen, letting us figure out if the ball will be bouncing up or down at the final bounce, and then applying some relatively simple math to estimate where said ball with finally ‘impact’ the left of right borders of the screen.
  • To accomplish this, the ‘y’ and ‘x’ times are calculated by dividing the ‘y’ and ‘x’ distances to be covered to their respective borders by the ‘y’ and ‘x’ velocities of the ball. Then, set, predetermined distances and times are subtracted from these values to determined how far, and so how many times, the ball will bounce, will the leftovers then being utilized to determine the final ‘goal’ coordinates.
  • Overall, this prevents the paddles from needlessly chasing after the ball. Instead, the AI only incurs a select few calculations, ideally occurring only a handful of times within its allotted window of only whenever when the ball passes onto a respective side of the playing field, and the paddles avoid moving unnecessarily.

Conclusion

  • The new Predictive Opponent AI is by no means perfect and still relatively crude, with a revisit of the mathematics behind its calculations being warranted, but otherwise functions as desired. It engages in far fewer movements than its simpler counterpart and yet can still put up a reasonable fight whilst in a controlled environment, as was desired. Further testing and tweaking will come to pass to clear up the aforementioned issues, mostly to reduce the copy and pasting of code when it should be possible to make calculations for both the top and bottom portions of the screen with very similar code.
