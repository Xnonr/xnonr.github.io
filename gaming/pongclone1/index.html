AI Prediction Modeling Within Pong Clone

Using Machine Learning Within R Language

Goal

  • To develop a smarter, more efficient and ‘human like’ predictive player AI opponent.

Key Aspects

  • The current AI opponent in both the classic and advanced game modes ‘cheats’ in that it knows where the ball is located at all times, much like a human does with their eyesight, but can move perfectly as needed so as to never miss, unlike human mistakes and limited reaction times.
  • The AI has the paddle or paddles, depending on the mode, ‘chase’ after the ball in a simple fashion, hitting it once then returning to a sort of standby mode, yet still moving up or down depending on said ball’s current location within the game window, ensuring it can intercept with best possible haste.
  • The AI is only beatable due to artificial limits imposed upon the speed at which it can move the paddles every so often to more closely resemble human reaction times, otherwise it would be unbeatable when facing a human player opponent. Against itself, often a stalemate emerges, with neither side scoring any points of very few points yet ultimately still ending in a tie.

Theory

  • The purpose of this new AI is to limit the computer’s ‘cheating’ to a minimum, so all calculations are based off of the ball’s original location and velocity during a small window of time just as it crosses over the dividing line on the game field.
  • For now, we are only looking at the classic game mode, taking into consideration only the height at which the paddles must be upon their side of the scree or ‘field’ in order to prevent an opposing goal, intercepting and redirecting the ball. This limits the complexity of the problem to only 1 dimension of movement with reference to the paddles, but not the ball.
  • Predicting where the ball would ‘score’ if let through makes up the majority of the challenge. As of now, the speed of the ball in terms of its ‘x’ velocity is fixed, as well as when it is considered as having scored upon reaching an x value of either -390 or 390. Predicting the Y value contains the majority of the complexity.
  • If the time it takes for the ball to reach either the top or the bottom of the screen is longer than it does to reach either the right or left edges, then the math is simple. Simply take the y velocity of the ball, multiply it by the time its takes to reach the end of the field divined from the x velocity and add it to its starting y position and you are golden.
  • If it is the other way around, the prediction becomes much more difficult. Due to the different angles at which the ball can bounce off the player paddles, different y velocities can emerge. The ball could bounce, once, twice or several times across both the top and bottom borders of the field changing its y velocity each time.
  • The distance that the ball covers in y must therefore be found, allowing us to discovered how many times it will bounce, where it bounces last, either the top or bottom, letting us figure out if the ball will be bouncing up or down, and then applying some relatively simple math to estimate where said ball with finally ‘impact’ the right border of the screen.
  • Overall, this prevents the paddle from chasing the ball, it does only a few calculations ideally one time only whenever the ball passes onto a respective side of the playing field, and the paddles avoid moving unnecessarily.

Conclusion

  • The new predictive AI is by no means perfect and relatively crude, with a revisit of the mathematics behind its calculations being warranted, but otherwise functions as desired. It engages in far fewer movements than its simpler counterpart and yet can still put up a reasonable fight whilst in a controlled environment. Further testing and tweaking will come to pass to clear up the aforementioned issues.
